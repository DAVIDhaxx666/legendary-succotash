At around one-thirty in the morning, I stumbled across an AI fortune-telling app in the App Store.

The reviews were insane.

“Holy shit, this thing is accurate. It scared me so bad I booked a red-eye home and avoided a disaster.”

“I pissed off a client. Asked if my boss would fire me the next day. The app said no, and I really didn’t get fired. Creepy as hell.”

Naturally, I downloaded it right away.

In the text box, I typed:

When am I going to get filthy rich?

But my thumb slipped.

Instead of asking when I’d get rich, I accidentally typed a question asking when I’d drop dead.

I cursed under my breath and was just about to delete it when the AI replied instantly:

“Calculation complete. Time of death: 2:00 a.m. Cause: mauled to death by a bear. Countdown: 28 minutes, 17 seconds.”

I stared for half a second, then burst out laughing.

What kind of bullshit was that?

I lived downtown. The nearest wildlife park was miles and miles away. And I was on the twenty-third floor.

What, was a bear supposed to take the elevator up?

I rolled my eyes. “Garbage app. What a waste of time.”

I was just about to uninstall it when my phone started vibrating so hard it nearly jumped out of my hand.

It was my best friend, Jenny Parker.

Jenny always went to bed early. Why the hell was she calling me at this hour?

A bad feeling hit me out of nowhere, but I still answered.

Before I could even say hello, her panicked, tear-shaky voice came blasting through the speaker.

“Emily, something’s wrong! That new Himalayan brown bear at the zoo—the one you saw a few days ago—it escaped!”

My breathing hitched.

Suddenly, I had a very bad feeling.

“What? Wasn’t the enclosure locked? Where’s security?”

“I don’t know!” Jenny’s voice shook violently. “The cameras all went dead! The tracker says it’s in your area—and it’s still moving!” She sucked in a breath, then added fast, “Didn’t you say you were working late tonight? Please tell me you stayed at the office.”

My stomach dropped.

I had been supposed to work late.

But it got canceled, and I came home.

If I’d known this shit was going to happen, I never would’ve left work.

When I didn’t answer, Jenny went quiet for a second, then asked in a strange, hollow voice:

“You’re already home… aren’t you?”

Then she rushed to reassure me.

“The rescue team is on the way. My brother, Caleb, is with the fire department—he went too.”

“Don’t panic. Lock your door. Don’t make a sound. Don’t turn on the lights. You’ll be okay…”

I answered her numbly, but my hands and feet had already turned ice-cold. I could barely hear the rest.

Because all I could think about was the app.

2:00 a.m. Mauled to death by a bear.

And then, all at once, I remembered the visit I’d made to Jenny’s zoo a few days ago.

That bear had been huge. Wrong huge.

It had sat quietly in the corner of the enclosure, staring at me without blinking. The iron bars in front of it had looked weirdly thin, almost fragile.

I’d shivered and joked to Jenny, “You sure this cage is enough? That thing looks like it could bend the bars if it really wanted to.”

“And when it stood up with its back turned to me? I swear to God, for a second it looked like a person.”

Jenny had laughed and called me paranoid.

“This enclosure is reinforced alloy. It’s solid. And that bear’s been calm the whole time.”

Then she smirked.

“Looks like somebody watches too many horror movies.”

Now, thinking back on it, my throat went dry.

I was just about to ask her if the bear was smart enough to figure out elevators—because if it was only wandering around the landscaping outside the building, maybe I didn’t need to lose my mind yet—

when I heard it.

Three heavy knocks on my front door.

My whole body locked up.

The phone nearly slipped from my hand.

In the dead silence of the night, the sound was wrong. Too loud. Too deliberate.

Even Jenny heard it through the phone.

“Emily? What was that? What’s happening over there?”

I stood frozen, one hand clamped over my mouth, too scared to answer.

Then survival instinct kicked in.

I flattened myself against the wall and moved slowly toward the door. On tiptoe, I pressed one eye to the peephole.

Under the dim hallway light, a massive figure stood outside my apartment, filling almost the entire lens.

It was wearing a yellow-and-blue food delivery jacket. There was a helmet on its head, visor pulled down low enough that I couldn’t see the face.

Then I smelled it.

A heavy, rank, animal stench seeped through the crack under the door.

I gagged.

And then a voice came from outside.

Mechanical. Distorted. Almost human.

“Your delivery is here. Please open the door.”

For one insane second, I thought maybe my boyfriend, Ryan Cole, had ordered me late-night food.

But through the peephole, I could clearly see the delivery guy wasn’t holding anything.

Then Jenny’s voice dropped to a whisper so sharp it cut right through me.

“Emily, do not open that door. That’s the bear.”

My blood turned to ice.

“It’s incredibly smart,” she said. “It can imitate human speech. Back away from the door. Right now.”

My knees nearly gave out.

I stumbled backward and half-crawled into my bedroom, clutching my phone so hard it hurt.

So this was it?

This was the bear from the app’s prediction?

The one that was supposed to kill me?

The clock was crawling toward two in the morning. My voice shook so badly I could barely get the words out.

“Jenny, what do I do? It’s outside my door. It’s knocking. It can talk…”

“When will the rescue team get here? What if it breaks in?”

She sounded like she was trying not to cry.

“Caleb’s team is close. Really close. Ten minutes at most.”

“Listen to me. That bear is smart, and it’s strong as hell, but it probably can’t get through a steel security door. As long as you don’t open it, it can’t get in.”

“Hide somewhere solid. Caleb’s team brought tranquilizer guns. They’ll stop it.”

I wanted to believe her.

I really did.

But then the knocking stopped.

The peephole went black.

Nothing. No movement. No shape.

What was it doing?

Looking for another way in?

I couldn’t just sit there and trust my life to one rusty apartment door and a rescue team that wasn’t here yet.

That was when I thought of the app again.

A crazy idea flashed through my mind.

If it could predict my death… Could it tell me how to survive?

I remembered the reviews. People saying it had helped them avoid disaster.

Fuck it.

At that point, I would’ve tried anything.

So I opened the app again and typed:

There’s a bear outside my door. How do I stay alive? Tell me now.

It answered instantly.

“Emergency survival plan generated.”

“Option 1: Create loud noise to attract neighbors and trigger group intervention.”

“Option 2: Climb from your window to the neighboring balcony.”

“Option 3: Use flammable household materials to make a crude incendiary weapon and throw it to drive the threat away.”

I stared at the screen in disbelief.

Make noise?

At this hour? Most of my neighbors were ordinary nine-to-fivers. Even if I woke them up, who the hell would come outside and face a bear that could stand upright and mimic human voices? And if I pissed it off?

Climb out the window to the next balcony?

I lived on the twenty-third floor. It was pitch-black outside, and it was drizzling. Looking down made me dizzy. Climbing out there was basically just choosing suicide with extra steps.

As for a Molotov cocktail—yeah, I had half a bottle of vodka and a few cans of hairspray.

But even if I made one, how the hell was I supposed to throw it through a locked door?

And if it didn’t scare the bear off? If I set my own apartment on fire instead?

This wasn’t a survival plan.

It was a fucking death sentence.

The app wasn’t helping me.

It was pushing me toward something even worse.

And then—

BANG.

Not knocking.

Impact.

The door shook violently.

The bear had started slamming into it.

Hard.

My old steel door rattled in its frame, sounding way less solid than it was supposed to.

I lost it and screamed.

On the phone, Jenny shouted, “Emily! What happened?”

My voice broke apart.

“It—it’s hitting the door…”

“Jenny, where’s Caleb? It sounds like it’s getting in…”

“Almost there!” she cried. “He said they’re downstairs already! Emily, just hold on—”

Then I heard the low-battery warning from her end.

A second later, the call died.

The pounding outside stopped too.

Everything went silent.

Then I heard slow, heavy footsteps outside my apartment.

Something was moving away from the door.

Hope flickered in my chest.

Maybe the rescue team had arrived.

Maybe they’d scared it off.

But the silence only lasted a few seconds.

Then I heard a short, muffled sound through the crack under the door.

Like someone choking on their own breath.

And right after that came the smell.

Fresh blood.

Thick and metallic.

I froze.

Had Caleb’s team subdued the bear?

Gathering what little courage I had left, I crept closer to the door and whispered:

“Caleb? Is that you?”

No response.

Then the silence broke.

Crunch. Tear. Wet chewing.

The sound of teeth ripping through flesh.

Something warm slid under the gap beneath the door.

I looked down.

Dark liquid.

Blood.

My stomach lurched so hard I thought I’d pass out.

Sweat soaked through my sleep shirt in seconds.

Caleb.

Jenny’s younger brother.

The firefighter who’d trailed after us as a kid and called me “Em” with that goofy little grin.

Had the bear just eaten him outside my door?

I collapsed against the wall and slid to the floor.

Fear and despair swallowed me whole.

There were only five minutes left until two.

Was I really going to die exactly the way the app had said?

Pale and shaking, I staggered toward the window, ready to try the app’s insane balcony-climbing plan.

At least if I fell, it would be quick.

Then my phone buzzed again.

I glanced down.

It was a notification from the couples app Ryan and I both used. It tracked our location when we shared status.

The distance between us was shrinking fast.

Less than five hundred meters.

I frowned.

Ryan was supposed to be out of town for work.

Why was he nearby?

And then the phone rang.

I answered instantly.

Before he could say anything, I shouted, “Ryan! Don’t come upstairs! There’s a bear in the hallway!”

“It’s really smart—it can mimic people. If you come up here, you’ll die!”

There was a short silence.

Then Ryan spoke, irritation already creeping into his voice.

“Emily, are you half-asleep? What bear? We’re downtown.”

“I got back from my trip early. I figured you’d still be awake, so I picked up your favorite late-night food and came to surprise you. I’m already by the elevator.” His tone sharpened.

“And now you’re pulling this?”

A beat.

“Who’s in there with you?”

I almost choked.

“What? No one!”

“Bullshit,” he snapped. “So what, I’m not convenient tonight? Is there some guy in your apartment?”

“Ryan, no!” I was crying now. “I’m telling the truth! Jenny can prove it! A bear escaped from her zoo—it’s outside my apartment! It was banging on my door. Jenny’s brother already—”

My voice broke.

Ryan let out a cold, disbelieving laugh.

“Jenny? The same Jenny who tried to set you up with her brother a while back? Yeah, super trustworthy.”

“Emily, we’re about to get engaged. And you’re standing there in the middle of the night feeding me this bullshit?”

His voice turned ugly.

“If you’re hiding some asshole in there, I’m coming up to see exactly what kind of bastard he is.”

I was almost hysterical.

“There is no one here! If you don’t believe me, I’ll send you proof!”

“The escape is on the news already. I’m sending it right now!”

With shaking hands, I sent him screenshots and links as fast as I could.

This time, when he spoke again, his tone had softened a little.

“Okay. I saw it.”

Then, more quietly:

“But Emily, I’m your boyfriend. How am I supposed to leave you in danger alone?”

“You don’t understand,” I said. “That thing is smart and insanely strong. If you come up here, you’re dead.”

“I have a stun baton in the car,” he said. “Let’s see how tough this bear really is.”

I felt sick.

“Ryan, are you insane? Don’t do this!”

But he ignored me.

“I’m not leaving you alone. Wait for me.” Then I heard the elevator doors open.

“Ryan, listen to me—”

The call cut off.

I slid to the floor, useless and numb.

Outside, the chewing had stopped.

The hallway was silent again.

Minute after minute passed.

I kept calling Ryan back.

No answer.

Again.

No answer.

Again.

Nothing.

Just when I was close to breaking, the phone lit up.

Ryan was calling.

I answered instantly, tears spilling down my face.

“Ryan! Oh my God—you scared me to death. I thought the bear got you—”

Silence.

Then Ryan’s voice came through.

Playful. Familiar. Almost laughing.

“See? I told you. There’s no bear in the city. And one that talks? Come on. Only you would fall for something like that.”

“It was just Jenny messing with you. There’s nothing outside your door.”

Then, warmly:

“Open up, Em. I brought your favorite crawfish.”

I froze.

My tears stopped cold.

A prank?

There was no bear?

Jenny had made the whole thing up?

Because if there really had been a bear outside, how could Ryan be talking like this?

The tension in my chest loosened a little.

Maybe this was all some horrible misunderstanding.

I reached for the lock.

And then another call came in.

Unknown number.

I answered, confused.

Jenny’s voice exploded through the speaker.

“Emily, don’t open the door! We’re in the stairwell with the rescue team. The person outside your apartment is not Ryan!”

My whole body went rigid.

Jenny was breathing hard, talking fast.

“Do not open the door. The thing outside just copied Ryan’s voice. It’s the bear.”

My mouth went dry. “What… what are you saying?”

“That bear is too smart,” she said, voice shaking. “It must’ve been hiding nearby, listening to your call with Ryan. It copied his tone, his words—everything. It’s trying to trick you into opening the door.”

I stopped breathing.

Jenny hesitated for one terrible second.

“Ryan might already…”

“No.” The word tore out of me. “No.”

“I warned him before he came upstairs,” I said, trembling violently. “He had a stun baton—”

“Listen to me!” Jenny was crying now. “If you don’t believe me, ask it something only you and Ryan would know. Something private. Right now.”

“Do that, and you’ll know the truth.”

The line went dead.

Outside, “Ryan” seemed to be losing patience.

The knocking started again—faster this time, heavier, with a thread of irritation under it.

“Emily? What are you doing in there? You really mad at me?”

Then that same familiar, teasing tone:

“Come on, open up. It’s the middle of the night. If you don’t hurry, I’m eating all the crawfish myself.”

It sounded so much like him.

Too much like him.

The same lazy drawl. The same half-joking complaint. The same affectionate little edge that had made me soften a thousand times before.

For one weak, stupid second, I nearly believed it.

Sweat slid down my back.

I forced myself to step closer to the door and made my voice light, even a little playful.

“Ryan… if you got crawfish, where’s my chocolate ice cream? Don’t tell me you forgot again.”

Outside, silence.

Not long. Just a few seconds.

Then “Ryan” answered, with forced good humor.

“Baby, I came in a rush this time. I forgot, okay? Don’t be mad. I’ll order some right now. Whatever you want.”

The world seemed to stop.

I went cold all over.

I was severely allergic to chocolate.

Ryan knew that better than anyone.

On our third anniversary, he’d once brought me a fancy box of chocolates by mistake. I’d broken out in hives and ended up in the hospital. He’d stayed beside my bed all night, blaming himself over and over.

There was no way in hell he would ever “forget.”

The thing outside was not Ryan.

It was the bear.

It had tricked me with his voice.

A tidal wave of terror and grief hit me so hard my legs gave out. I dropped to the floor, slamming my back against the wall.

If that wasn’t Ryan outside my door—

Then where was the real Ryan?

I couldn’t finish the thought.

I tried to look through the peephole again, but all I saw was darkness.

At some point, the motion-sensor hallway light had gone out.

Then suddenly, chaos erupted outside.

A heavy crash.

Something hitting the wall.

Something falling.

A short, savage roar.

And then, just as suddenly, silence again.

The blood smell flooded in under the door, so thick it made me gag.

I curled into myself on the floor, shaking, tears pouring down my face.

I don’t know how much time passed before I heard another voice outside.

This time, it was Jenny.

Calm—too calm. Forced calm. Underneath it, I could hear a tremor.

“Emily? Open the door. It’s okay now. The bear’s hurt. We drove it off. It won’t last long.”

“You’re safe now.”

I didn’t move.

Didn’t answer.

A minute later, she called again, more urgently.

“Emily! Say something! Don’t do this to me! Open the door—it’s really over!”

Safe?

After everything that had happened, I didn’t trust anyone anymore.

That bear was too smart.

What if this was another trick?

What if this was just another voice, another trap, another lie meant to lure me out?

And underneath all that fear, one question kept circling in my head like a vulture:

Why me?

I’d only seen that animal once at the zoo.

Why would it travel all that way to come after me?

And what the hell had that AI app really been?

I sat in the corner with my knees pulled to my chest, my back pressed hard against the wall, my eyes fixed on the door until dawn finally started to show through the window.

Morning light.

At last.

Every muscle in my body felt dead as I dragged myself to the entrance and unlocked the door with shaking hands.

The smell hit me first.

Blood.

So much blood it nearly blacked out my vision.

The hallway was a wreck.

Dark red stains splashed across the walls and floor, half-dried and smeared in ugly streaks.

Jenny sat collapsed on the stairs a few yards away, white as paper, face streaked with tears and blood. Her eyes were swollen nearly shut.

Next to her, Caleb was half-kneeling.

Alive.

His arm was wrapped in a rough emergency bandage, and he looked like hell, but when he saw me, he pushed himself up and said hoarsely:

“Em… thank God you’re okay.”

But I was already staring past him.

Near the elevator, in the deepest pool of blood, I saw a familiar dark blue phone case.

And half of a crushed takeout container.

Crawfish.

My stomach turned inside out.

I clapped a hand over my mouth and gagged.

Caleb swallowed hard. “Ryan… he…”

“He’s not dead!” Jenny suddenly shouted, jerking her head up. “Emily, Ryan’s not dead! We checked the elevator camera!”

Her words came out in a mess, but little by little, the story formed.

Ryan had stepped out of the elevator and walked straight into the bear, which had been lying in wait in a blind corner of the hallway.

He’d reacted fast—pulled out the stun baton and fought.

In that narrow corridor, man and bear went at each other in a brutal struggle. At some point Ryan had managed to hit it.

But the bear, enraged, had swatted him hard enough to throw him into the wall.

He’d gone down unconscious.

And then came the part that made my skin crawl.

The bear hadn’t eaten him there.

It had dragged him away.

And before it disappeared, it had even picked up the crawfish container he’d dropped.

Fire and police had traced the blood trail and security footage. The bear had dragged Ryan down the stairwell, out of the building, and vanished near the edge of the city.

It was wounded, but still moving fast.

And according to the last bit of footage—

Ryan had still been breathing.

Alive.

Jenny handed me a blurry still from the surveillance video.

In the image, the bear was dragging Ryan’s unconscious body toward the shadows of the stairwell.

Its eyes, caught in the dim grain of the camera, seemed fixed directly on the lens.

There was something in that stare that made my chest tighten.

Not random animal rage.

Not hunger.

Purpose.

Hatred.

Jenny looked at me uneasily.

“Emily, we checked all the zoo footage. Ever since the day you visited, that bear had been acting… wrong.”

“It kept watching you. Constantly. But not like a predator watching prey.”

She paused, then looked at my neck.

“It looked like it was staring at something you were wearing.”

My hand moved there automatically.

A small pendant hung from a faded red cord around my neck—a yellowed piece of bone no bigger than my thumbnail, its edges worn smooth with age.

My father had brought it back years ago, when I was little.

He’d been doing animal behavior research in Tibet—well, in the Himalayan region of western China and Nepal—and something had gone wrong during the expedition. His team got lost in a blizzard and was attacked by an unusually aggressive adult bear. In the chaos, they were forced to kill it.

Later, one of the local guides had taken this tiny piece of bone from the dead animal and said it could serve as a token of appeasement or compensation.

My dad had put it on a cord and hung it around my neck after he got home. I’d been a sickly kid, always burning with fevers, and he told my mom it might help “settle my nerves.”

A horrible idea began to form.

Absurd.

Impossible.

And yet—

three days later, the bear was finally cornered in a cave beneath a cliff outside the city.

A professional tranquilizer team brought it down.

It was covered in injuries, especially one ugly blackened wound on its hind leg from Ryan’s stun baton. Even under sedation, it had fought like hell before finally collapsing.

Then the rescue team went into the cave.

They carried Ryan out alive.

Barely.

His clothes were torn to rags. He’d lost terrifying amounts of weight in only a few days. His eyes were vacant, unfocused, almost dead.

When people called his name, he didn’t respond.

He just shook. Violently, uncontrollably.

He wouldn’t let anyone come near him. He cried, laughed, muttered to himself.

Whatever that bear had done to him in that cave—

it hadn’t just kept him there as food.

Looking at him on the stretcher, twitching and hollow-eyed, no trace left of the man I knew, I couldn’t stop the sigh that escaped me.

A few days later, the bear was returned to the zoo and placed in the highest-security isolation enclosure they had—newly reinforced, thoroughly inspected, impossible to breach.

It was badly injured and spent most of its time hidden in a corner.

I went to see it.

I stood behind the thick reinforced glass with that tiny bone pendant clenched in my hand.

The moment the bear sensed it, its eyes flew open.

Then it lunged.

Dragging its injured leg, it hurled itself at the glass with terrifying speed. Its massive paw slammed against the barrier hard enough to make the whole viewing room tremble.

Its eyes locked onto the bone in my hand.

And then it let out a sound that didn’t sound like rage.

It sounded like pain.

Real pain.

Tears welled in its eyes—huge, visible tears—and rolled through the fur of its face.

It started smashing its head against the glass, over and over, never once looking away from that little fragment of bone.

And in that moment, everything clicked.

The miles it had traveled.

The relentless pursuit.

The way it found me.

The almost human obsession.

Its hatred had never been about me.

It had been about what I was wearing.

About the thing hanging around my neck.

This wasn’t some random attack.

This was revenge.

Or grief.

Maybe both.

Maybe that tiny piece of bone had belonged to one of its own.

A cub. A mate. Its mother.

I’d never know.

My hands shaking, I opened the small transfer hatch built into the outer enclosure wall while Jenny and the keepers watched from a distance.

I slid the pendant through.

The little bone landed softly on the straw inside.

At once, the bear went still.

Completely still.

Then, slowly—almost reverently—it lowered its massive body to the ground and touched the bone with unbelievable care.

A broken, aching whine came from deep in its throat.

Using both forepaws, it gathered the bone to its chest and curled around it, pressing its head down over it like it was shielding something sacred.

Its shoulders trembled.

Whatever that fragment had once been, it had belonged to blood.

To family.

And the truth of it was probably buried forever in some frozen mountain years and years ago.

The zoo underwent a full investigation and overhaul. Security systems were upgraded. Enclosures were reinforced. Protocols changed.

Ryan was transferred to the best psychiatric rehabilitation center in the city.

The doctors said he’d suffered extreme terror and severe psychological trauma. Recovery would be long, uncertain, maybe impossible.

Sometimes, when he looked at me, I thought I saw a flicker of recognition.

Just a flicker.

Then it would drown again beneath fear and confusion.

He didn’t remember the bear.

He didn’t remember what happened in the cave.

Eventually, it became clear he barely remembered me, either.

Watching him curl up in his hospital bed, flinching at birds outside the window, I knew the man who used to bicker with me, tease me, and make plans for our wedding was gone.

I canceled the engagement quietly.

Returned the ring to his family.

No screaming. No dramatic collapse.

Just the bone-deep exhaustion of someone who survived something she still didn’t fully understand.

I moved out of that twenty-third-floor apartment as fast as I could.

That door—the one that had held death back for a few extra hours.

That hallway—the one where the bear had knocked in borrowed voices.

Every inch of that place turned into nightmare fuel the second the sun went down.

The bone pendant—the thing that had triggered all of it—I gave to Jenny and asked her to return it to the zoo.

It was never mine to keep.

Whatever should be done with it, it belonged with the animal it had nearly destroyed me trying to reclaim.

As for the AI fortune app—

while packing for the move, I stared at that cursed icon on my phone for a long time.

Then I deleted it.

Permanently.

The screen went dark.

Outside my new place was a different neighborhood, a different street, a different life. Bright sun. Cars passing. People laughing.

I took a long breath and told myself that if enough time passed, the nightmare would finally fade.

Being alive was luck enough.

The future, whatever it was, would have to be walked one step at a time.

By me.

A year later, on another weekend after midnight, I found myself doom-scrolling again.

That was when I stumbled into some occult livestream.

The gimmick was simple: send one beer gift, get one fortune read.

The stream only had a few hundred viewers, but the comments were packed with praise.

“Wow, this guy is freakishly accurate! I followed his advice and found my dog after it had been missing for almost a month!”

“No joke, he told me I’d have a blood-related disaster today and warned me not to take my usual route to work. That road collapsed this morning.”

“Is this real? I’m sending ten beers. Can the master tell me how to get a girlfriend?”

My life had gone back to the usual endless grind. Work, bills, exhaustion, repeat.

The curiosity crept in again.

Against my better judgment, I sent one beer.

Then I typed the same question I’d once tried to ask the app.

Only this time, I checked it three times to make sure there was no typo.

When will I finally get rich?

The fortune teller’s face changed as soon as he read it.

His brows pulled together sharply.

Then he leaned toward the camera and said, urgent and serious:

“Forget getting rich for a second, young lady.”

“I’ve made the calculation, and you’re facing a bloody catastrophe very soon.”

“If you don’t resolve it immediately…”

He paused.

“…you may not survive tonight.”

My vision went black at the edges.

I had spent a whole year trying to bury what happened with the bear.

And just like that, every shadow of it came rushing back.

The fear.

The voices.

The knocking.

The smell of blood under the door.

The nightmare had not forgotten me.
